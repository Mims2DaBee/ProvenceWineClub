The Provençal Table: 15 Foods to Pair with Rosé

A menu of salty, herbal, tomato-rich and sea-bright dishes that show why Provence rosé belongs at the table.

In brief: Provence rosé is most useful with Mediterranean flavours—olive oil, tomato, seafood, herbs and garlic. Match delicate wines to lighter dishes and structured wines to grilled or slow-cooked food.

Apéritif · 01–05

Small plates and starters

Salty, crisp and generous—the dishes that make the first glass disappear.

  1. Tapenade

    Dry, herbal rosé refreshes the salty olive paste.

  2. Pissaladière

    Choose enough fruit for sweet onion and enough acidity for anchovy and olive.

  3. Panisse

    Crisp chickpea fries welcome a fresh, citrus-led style.

  4. Goat cheese

    Acidity cuts through creamy texture while herbal notes meet the rind.

  5. Petits farcis

    Stuffed Provençal vegetables suit a medium-bodied rosé.

From the coast · 06–10

Seafood and coastal dishes

Think saline freshness, citrus lift and enough structure for smoke, saffron and garlic.

  1. Oysters

    Choose the driest, most delicate wine.

  2. Grilled sardines

    A savoury wine handles oil, smoke and sea.

  3. Bouillabaisse

    A structured rosé meets saffron, tomato, garlic and rouille.

  4. Salade Niçoise

    Bright acidity ties together tuna, tomato, anchovy and olives.

  5. Grilled prawns

    Grenache and Cinsault fruit works with shellfish sweetness.

Garden & hills · 11–15

Vegetables and larger plates

For tomato, herbs and the grill, move towards rosé with body, texture and savoury depth.

  1. Ratatouille

    Herbal, red-fruited rosé mirrors tomato and summer vegetables.

  2. Tomates provençales

    Choose high freshness for ripe tomato, garlic and breadcrumbs.

  3. Aioli garni

    Rosé refreshes between garlic aioli, vegetables, egg and fish.

  4. Herb-roasted chicken

    A textured style stands up to crisp skin and rosemary.

  5. Lamb with thyme

    Reach for a fuller, savoury, Mourvèdre-influenced rosé.

The one rule

Build the pairing from the sauce

When adapting these dishes with Australian produce, match the wine to the strongest element on the plate. Lemon and raw shellfish need freshness; tomato and saffron need fruit and body; char and garlic need savoury depth. The protein alone rarely decides the bottle.

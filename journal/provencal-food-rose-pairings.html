The Provençal Table: 15 Foods to Pair with Rosé

A menu of salty, herbal, tomato-rich and sea-bright dishes that show why Provence rosé belongs at the table.

In brief: Provence rosé is most useful with Mediterranean flavours—olive oil, tomato, seafood, herbs and garlic. Match delicate wines to lighter dishes and structured wines to grilled or slow-cooked food.

Small plates and starters

  1. Tapenade: dry, herbal rosé refreshes the salty olive paste.
  2. Pissaladière: choose enough fruit for sweet onion and enough acidity for anchovy and olive.
  3. Panisse: crisp chickpea fries welcome a fresh citrus-led style.
  4. Goat cheese: acidity cuts through creamy texture while herbal notes meet the rind.
  5. Petits farcis: stuffed Provençal vegetables suit a medium-bodied rosé.

Seafood and coastal dishes

  1. Oysters: choose the driest, most delicate wine.
  2. Grilled sardines: a savoury wine handles oil, smoke and sea.
  3. Bouillabaisse: a structured rosé meets saffron, tomato, garlic and rouille.
  4. Salade Niçoise: bright acidity ties together tuna, tomato, anchovy and olives.
  5. Grilled prawns: Grenache and Cinsault fruit works with shellfish sweetness.

Vegetables and larger plates

  1. Ratatouille: herbal, red-fruited rosé mirrors tomato and summer vegetables.
  2. Tomates provençales: choose high freshness for ripe tomato, garlic and breadcrumbs.
  3. Aioli garni: rosé refreshes the palate between garlic aioli, vegetables, egg and fish.
  4. Herb-roasted chicken: a textured style stands up to crisp skin and rosemary.
  5. Lamb with thyme: reach for a fuller, savoury, Mourvèdre-influenced rosé.

Build the pairing from the sauce

When adapting these dishes with Australian produce, match the wine to the strongest element on the plate. Lemon and raw shellfish need freshness; tomato and saffron need fruit and body; char and garlic need savoury depth. The protein alone rarely decides the bottle.

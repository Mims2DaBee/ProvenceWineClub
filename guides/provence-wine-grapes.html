Inside the blend

Provence Wine Grapes Explained

The varieties behind Provence rosé, red and white wine—and what each can contribute to your glass.

In brief: Provence wine is usually about blends, not one signature grape. Grenache often brings body and fruit; Cinsault freshness and delicacy; Mourvèdre structure and spice; Syrah colour and backbone; and Tibouren distinctive aromatic character. For white wine, Rolle—Vermentino in Australia—is the key name to remember.

A grape description is a useful clue, not a tasting guarantee. Site, ripeness, winemaking and the proportions in a blend all change the result. Appellation rules also differ, so a variety permitted in one part of Provence may have a different role—or no role—in another.

The main grapes for Provence rosé and red wine

Grenache

Grenache can bring body, generosity and red-fruit character. In young wines that may mean fresh berry notes; with greater ripeness or development, the profile can become spicier and more savoury. It is familiar to Australians through Grenache and GSM wines.

Cinsault

Cinsault is valued for freshness, delicacy and fruit. In a rosé blend it can soften more powerful varieties and help create a graceful shape. Australian drinkers may also see the spelling Cinsaut.

Mourvèdre

Mourvèdre is late ripening and particularly at home in warm sites near the sea. It can contribute structure, tannin, dark fruit, violet and spice. In Bandol it has a defining role, especially in age-worthy red, and can also give rosé more depth. Australians may know it as Mataro.

Syrah

Syrah can add colour, tannin and savoury structure, with red or dark fruit and peppery notes depending on site and treatment. In Australia the same variety is usually called Shiraz, though the name alone does not make the resulting styles equivalent.

Tibouren

Tibouren has a particularly Provençal identity and is closely associated with rosé. It is often described as bringing aromatic, fine and spicy character. Because it is far less common in Australia, seeing it in a producer's technical sheet is a useful signal to look more closely.

Carignan and other partners

Carignan can contribute colour, acidity, tannin and savoury character, particularly to red blends. Cabernet Sauvignon appears in some appellations, while lesser-known varieties such as Rousseli can play specific roles. Treat the appellation's current rules as authoritative.

The white grapes to know

Rolle (Vermentino)

Rolle is the Provençal name for the grape Australians generally know as Vermentino. Official Vins de Provence descriptions associate it with citrus, pear, almond and fennel, as well as texture and balance. It is the clearest bridge for an Australian drinker exploring Provence white wine.

Clairette

Clairette is a long-established southern variety. It can contribute white-fruit, fennel and floral notes. You may encounter it in white blends from Provence and neighbouring Mediterranean regions.

Ugni Blanc, Sémillon and Grenache Blanc

Ugni Blanc can support freshness; Sémillon can add texture and breadth; Grenache Blanc can contribute body and ripe-fruit character. Their permitted use depends on the appellation. Cassis, Côtes de Provence and Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence do not share one universal recipe.

Why the blend matters more than a grape checklist

Blending can balance acidity, alcohol, aroma, colour, texture and structure while helping a wine express its place and intended use. Grenache and Cinsault may form a fresh, supple core; Mourvèdre or Syrah may add grip; a small proportion of another permitted variety may shift aroma or balance.

Rosé is not simply a pale version of the producer's red. Picking decisions, skin contact, pressing, fermentation and ageing are chosen for the intended wine. Read the complete Provence rosé guide for direct pressing and short maceration.

Grape character is not a promise

“Grenache tastes like…” can be a helpful starting phrase, but it leaves out place and process. Use varieties to form a question about a wine, not to predetermine the answer.

Choose a Provence wine by the style you want

  • Light, fresh rosé: look for balance and provenance first; Grenache and Cinsault are common reference points.
  • Rosé for a full meal: ask about structure, texture and the role of Mourvèdre, Syrah or Tibouren rather than choosing by colour.
  • Structured red: explore Mourvèdre-led Bandol or appellation-specific southern blends.
  • Mediterranean white: begin with Rolle-led wine or seek the coastal white wines of Cassis.

Quick answers

What grapes are used in Provence rosé?

Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah, Mourvèdre and Tibouren are among the important black grapes. Exact permissions and proportions depend on the appellation.

What is Rolle called in Australia?

Rolle is Vermentino. Rolle is the traditional name you are likely to encounter in Provence; Vermentino is more familiar on Australian labels.

Is Provence rosé usually a blend?

Yes. Blending lets producers balance fruit, freshness, body, aroma and structure while meeting the relevant appellation rules.

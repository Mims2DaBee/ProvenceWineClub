Provence Wine Appellations Compared

Nine names, three wine colours and one practical question: which Provence bottle suits your table in Australia?

In brief: Provence is the region; Côtes de Provence is one appellation within it. For fresh, classic rosé, begin with Côtes de Provence, Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence or Coteaux Varois en Provence. For more structured rosé or Mourvèdre-led red, look to Bandol. For distinctive coastal white wine, remember Cassis.

Appellation names are not a quality ladder. They define origin and production rules, but each contains different sites and producers. Use the name as a style clue, then consider the producer, cuvée, vintage and how the bottle has travelled and been stored in Australia.

The three appellations represented by Vins de Provence

Côtes de Provence

The largest and most internally varied of the region's appellations extends across the Var and Bouches-du-Rhône and into one commune in Alpes-Maritimes. Limestone country lies mainly to the north and west; crystalline massifs influence the south and east. That scale is why “Côtes de Provence” should never be treated as one flavour.

Five additional geographic designations identify more specific terroirs: Sainte-Victoire, Fréjus, La Londe, Pierrefeu and Notre-Dame des Anges. Read one on a label as a more precise origin within Côtes de Provence.

Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence

The westernmost of the three stretches from the Durance toward the Mediterranean and from the Rhône side toward Montagne Sainte-Victoire. Sun and the Mistral shape the landscape. Rosé is most visible in Australia, but red and white are also made.

Coteaux Varois en Provence

This inland appellation sits in limestone Provence around Brignoles. Its vineyards occupy basins and narrow valleys at generally higher elevations than much of the coast. For buyers, the useful clue is freshness rather than a fixed aroma.

Why “Vins de Provence” is not the whole region

The regional council represents the three appellations above. Bandol, Cassis and the names below belong to wider Provence but are not interchangeable with those three.

The wider Provence names worth knowing

Bandol: structure and Mourvèdre

Bandol makes rosé, red and white. Its warm coastal setting suits late-ripening Mourvèdre, the grape that defines its famous reds and contributes depth to many rosés. Choose Bandol rosé when you want more table presence; choose Bandol red for structure and the potential to develop with time.

Cassis: coastal white wine

Cassis sits by the Mediterranean east of Marseille and is the Provence appellation to remember when white wine is the priority. Its whites make intuitive partners for shellfish, grilled fish, fennel and olive oil. Do not confuse the appellation with crème de cassis, the blackcurrant liqueur.

Palette, Les Baux-de-Provence, Bellet and Pierrevert

These smaller names show why Provence cannot be reduced to pale rosé. Palette, near Aix, is tiny. Les Baux-de-Provence lies around the Alpilles. Bellet occupies steep hills around Nice and uses distinctive local varieties. Pierrevert sits at the higher, northern edge of wider Provence. Australian availability is limited, but the names reward exploration on specialist lists.

Choose by occasion

Aperitif and a mixed crowd

Begin with a dry, fresh rosé from one of the three core Vins de Provence appellations.

Seafood lunch

Try Cassis white, a Rolle-led Provence white or a bright rosé. See the seafood guide.

Roast chicken, lamb or bouillabaisse

Look for textured rosé, including Bandol, or a savoury Provence red matched to the dish.

A curious wine-lover's gift

Choose a reputable producer from a smaller appellation and buy from a retailer who can explain provenance.

Three label traps

  1. Provence is not short for Côtes de Provence. The first is the wider region; the second a specific AOP.
  2. “Provence-style” is not protected origin. Australian rosé may be inspired by Provence without coming from France.
  3. Pale is not a quality grade. Colour cannot prove dryness, authenticity or careful transport.

Quick answers

What is the difference between Provence and Côtes de Provence?

Provence is the wider wine region. Côtes de Provence is one protected appellation within it.

Which Provence appellation is best for rosé?

There is no single best. Côtes de Provence offers breadth, Coteaux Varois en Provence can show inland freshness, and Bandol is a useful direction for more structure.

Which Provence appellation is known for white wine?

Cassis is especially associated with coastal white wine. White is also made in several other Provence appellations.

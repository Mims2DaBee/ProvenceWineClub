Provence Wine Glossary

The grapes, places, winemaking words and French label terms worth knowing, explained for wine drinkers in Australia.

In brief: AOP identifies a protected origin, not a flavour or quality score. Côtes de Provence, Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence and Coteaux Varois en Provence are the three appellations represented by Vins de Provence. Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah, Mourvèdre and Tibouren are important black grapes; Rolle is an important white grape.

French wine labels often lead with place rather than grape. This A–Z explains what the words mean, what they do not guarantee, and how they help when choosing Provence wine in Australia. Pronunciations are intentionally omitted: they vary, and understanding the bottle matters more than performing the accent.

A–CD–MP–ST–V

A–C

AOC / AOP
Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée is the traditional French term; Appellation d'Origine Protégée is the corresponding protected-origin term used at European level. On wine, it connects a defined place with production rules. It does not mean every bottle tastes alike or that one AOP automatically outranks another.
Assemblage
Blending. A winemaker may combine grape varieties, vineyard parcels or separately made lots to shape the finished wine. Many Provence rosés are blends: this is a deliberate part of the style, not a sign of lesser quality.
Bandol
A separate Provence AOP around the coastal town of Bandol, producing red, rosé and white wine. Mourvèdre is central to its identity, especially in red and rosé. Bandol sits in the wider Provence wine region but is not one of the three appellations represented under the Vins de Provence banner.
Cassis
A small coastal Provence appellation immediately east of Marseille, particularly associated with white wine. Do not confuse the place name with crème de cassis, the blackcurrant liqueur from Burgundy.
Cinsault
A black grape widely used in Provence rosé. It can contribute freshness, perfume, delicate fruit and softness in a blend. The name is also commonly written Cinsaut in official French documents.
Clairette
A long-established southern French white grape. In Provence it can bring white-fruit, floral, fennel and lime-blossom characters, depending on site and winemaking.
Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence
The westernmost of the three Vins de Provence appellations, extending between the Rhône side, the Durance, the Mediterranean and the Sainte-Victoire area. Rosé leads production, with red and white wines also made.
Coteaux Varois en Provence
An inland appellation centred around Brignoles in limestone Provence. Its higher, enclosed vineyard areas can experience cooler conditions than many coastal sites, helping preserve freshness.
Côtes de Provence
The largest and most geographically varied Provence appellation. It spans parts of the Var and Bouches-du-Rhône plus one commune in Alpes-Maritimes. Its name alone does not describe one uniform rosé style.
Cru Classé
A historic classification awarded in 1955 to selected Côtes de Provence estates. It refers to an estate, not a universal ranking of every cuvée or vintage, and should not be confused with classifications in Bordeaux.
Cuvée
A named bottling, blend or production lot. The word by itself is neutral: cuvée does not automatically mean premium, reserve or single vineyard.

D–M

DGC
Dénomination Géographique Complémentaire, an additional geographic designation within an appellation. Côtes de Provence currently recognises five: Sainte-Victoire, Fréjus, La Londe, Pierrefeu and Notre-Dame des Anges. Each has extra geographic and production requirements.
Direct pressing
A rosé method in which black grapes are pressed soon after harvest, limiting extraction from their skins. The juice is then fermented like a white wine. Gentle direct pressing often produces pale colour, but colour alone does not reveal the complete method or quality.
Domaine
Estate. On a label it usually indicates a wine-growing property, but the exact business and bottling arrangements still need to be read from the full label.
Grenache
A major Mediterranean black grape and an important component of Provence rosé and red blends. It can bring red fruit, body and warmth. In France, the black form may be written Grenache Noir.
Maceration
Contact between grape juice and skins. For rosé, a short, controlled period can extract colour, aroma and a little structure before the juice is separated and fermented. Longer contact generally extracts more, but grape, temperature and pressing also matter.
Mistral
The strong, often dry northerly wind associated with the Rhône valley and Provence. It can reduce humidity in vineyards and influence growing conditions, but exposure differs greatly across the region.
Mourvèdre
A late-ripening black grape suited to warm Mediterranean sites. It can bring structure, dark fruit, spice and savoury depth. It is especially important in Bandol, where it plays a defining role.

P–S

Palette
A tiny appellation near Aix-en-Provence producing red, rosé and white wines from a broad set of permitted varieties. It is geographically within Provence but distinct from Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence.
Provence-style rosé
A marketing description commonly used in Australia and elsewhere for pale, dry rosé inspired by Provence. Unless the wine comes from a protected Provence appellation, the phrase does not mean it is Provence wine. Check the stated origin on the label.
Rolle
The Provençal name for Vermentino, an important white grape in the region. It commonly contributes citrus, pear, almond, fennel, texture and freshness to white wines and, where appellation rules permit, to rosé blends.
Saignée
Literally “bleeding”: juice is drawn from a red-wine fermentation or maceration. It is one way to make rosé, but direct pressing and purpose-grown rosé production are especially important in Provence. Do not assume every darker rosé is saignée.
Syrah
A black grape used in Provence red and rosé blends. It can contribute colour, spice, dark fruit and structure. It is the same variety known as Shiraz in Australia, though regional style and winemaking can be very different.

T–V

Terroir
The interaction of place and human practice: climate, soil, slope, exposure, surrounding environment, grape growing and local knowledge. It is more useful as a framework than as a tasting note.
Tibouren
A historic black grape closely associated with Provence, especially rosé. It can add floral, herbal, red-fruit and earthy detail. It is usually part of a blend, though varietally focused examples exist.
Vendange
Harvest. Vendanges manuelles means hand harvesting; vendange mécanique means machine harvesting. Harvest method is only one factor in wine quality and should be interpreted with fruit condition and producer practice.
Vigneron / Vigneronne
A male or female winegrower. The term often implies involvement with the vines as well as wine production, though real roles vary between estates.
Vins de Provence
The regional wine council identity covering Côtes de Provence, Coteaux d'Aix-en-Provence and Coteaux Varois en Provence. It is not a synonym for every wine appellation located in the wider geographic region of Provence.

How this glossary is checked
